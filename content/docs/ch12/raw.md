# Components of Human Coordination <sub>[12.1.1]</sub>

## What's the Goal?
Coordination ensures that organisms can detect environmental changes and respond appropriately for survival and adaptation.

## The Breakdown
{{% steps %}}
### Step 1: Stimulus Detection
A **stimulus** (change in environment) occurs and is detected by specialized **receptors**.

### Step 2: Signal Conversion
Receptors convert the stimulus into nerve impulses that can travel through the nervous system.

### Step 3: Integration and Processing
The **integration centre** (brain or spinal cord) receives and interprets the nerve impulses.

### Step 4: Response Coordination
The integration centre sends instructions through motor neurones to the appropriate **effector**.

### Step 5: Response Execution
The **effector** (muscle or gland) produces the **response** to address the original stimulus.
{{% /steps %}}

# Types of Sensory Receptors <sub>[12.1.3]</sub>

## What is it?
Sensory receptors are specialized cells that detect specific types of stimuli and convert them into nerve impulses for processing by the nervous system.

## Key Characteristics
- Each receptor type is sensitive to a specific stimulus
- Located at nerve fiber endings
- Function as energy converters (convert stimulus energy to electrical signals)
- Found in both sensory organs and internal body locations

## Primary Functions
**Receptor Types and Their Stimuli:**
- **Photoreceptor**: Detects light (found in eyes)
- **Thermoreceptor**: Detects temperature changes (found in skin, hypothalamus)
- **Chemoreceptor**: Detects chemical substances (found in nose, tongue, blood vessels)
- **Baroreceptor**: Detects pressure changes (found in blood vessels)
- **Mechanoreceptor**: Detects touch and pressure (found in skin, inner ear)
- **Nociceptor**: Detects pain (found throughout body tissues)

# Nervous System Organization <sub>[12.2.1]</sub>

## What is it?
The human nervous system is a network of nerve cells (neurones) organized into two main divisions that work together to coordinate body functions.

## Key Characteristics

- Divided into central and peripheral components
- Uses electrical and chemical signals for communication
- Integrates information and coordinates responses

- **Brain**: Main control and coordination center
- **Spinal Cord**: Relay center and reflex control

**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):**
- **Sensory Receptors**: Detect stimuli
- **Cranial Nerves**: 12 pairs connecting brain to body parts
- **Spinal Nerves**: 31 pairs connecting spinal cord to body parts

# Spinal Cord Structure and Function <sub>[12.2.2]</sub>

## What is it?
The spinal cord is a cylindrical bundle of nerve tissue protected within the vertebral column, serving as the main pathway between the brain and peripheral nervous system.

## Key Characteristics
- Protected by vertebral column and cerebrospinal fluid
- Contains white matter (myelinated axons) and grey matter (cell bodies)
- Grey matter has butterfly or "H" shape in cross-section
- Spinal nerves extend through dorsal and ventral roots

## Primary Functions
- **Information Processing**: Handles certain sensory information and sends appropriate motor responses
- **Reflex Control**: Coordinates reflex actions without brain involvement
- **Communication Pathway**: Connects brain with peripheral nervous system
- **Integration Center**: Processes simple responses and complex reflexes

**Structural Components:**
- **Grey Matter**: Contains neurone cell bodies, appears butterfly-shaped
- **White Matter**: Contains myelinated axons, surrounds grey matter
- **Dorsal Root**: Carries sensory neurone axons into spinal cord
- **Ventral Root**: Carries motor neurone axons out of spinal cord

# Peripheral Nervous System Functions <sub>[12.2.3]</sub>

## What is it?
The peripheral nervous system is the network of nerves and receptors that connects the central nervous system to all parts of the body.

## Key Characteristics
- Consists of all nervous tissue outside the brain and spinal cord
- Includes 12 pairs of cranial nerves and 31 pairs of spinal nerves
- Divided into somatic and autonomic divisions
- Serves as communication bridge between CNS and body

## Primary Functions
**Somatic Nervous System:**
- Regulates all controlled (voluntary) actions
- Controls skeletal muscle movements
- Processes conscious sensory information

**Autonomic Nervous System:**
- Controls involuntary actions
- Manages heartbeat, blood vessel contraction, breathing
- Regulates glandular secretions
- Maintains homeostatic functions

**Overall PNS Function:**
- Connect sensory receptors to the central nervous system
- Relay motor commands from CNS to effectors
- Enable two-way communication throughout the body

# Basic Neurone Structure <sub>[12.3.1]</sub>

## What is it?
A neurone is a specialized nerve cell designed to transmit electrical and chemical signals throughout the nervous system.

## Key Characteristics
- Highly specialized cell with elongated projections
- Capable of generating and conducting electrical impulses
- Contains all typical cell components plus specialized structures
- Designed for rapid, long-distance communication

## Primary Functions
**Structural Components:**
- **Cell Body**: Contains nucleus and cytoplasm; integrates signals and coordinates metabolism
- **Dendrites**: Short branches that receive nerve impulses from other neurones or receptors
- **Axon**: Long projection that carries impulses away from cell body to other neurones or effectors
- **Myelin Sheath**: Insulating membrane that protects axon, speeds impulse transmission, and provides nutrients
- **Node of Ranvier**: Gaps in myelin sheath that allow impulses to "jump" and accelerate transmission
- **Synaptic Knob**: Swollen end of axon that releases chemical signals to next cell

# Synapse Structure and Components <sub>[12.3.3]</sub>

## What is it?
A synapse is the specialized junction between two neurones where nerve impulses are transmitted from one neurone to another.

## Key Characteristics
- Narrow gap separating synaptic knob from receiving neurone
- Contains specialized structures for chemical communication
- Allows one-way transmission of nerve impulses
- Controls which impulses are allowed to pass through

## Primary Functions
**Structural Components:**
- **Synaptic Knob**: Swollen end of transmitting neurone containing neurotransmitters
- **Synaptic Vesicles**: Small sacs storing neurotransmitter chemicals
- **Synapse Gap**: Narrow space between neurones (about 20-50 nanometers)
- **Receptor Proteins**: Specialized proteins on receiving neurone that bind to neurotransmitters
- **Mitochondria**: Abundant in synaptic knob to provide energy for transmission

**Key Functions:**
- Enable communication between neurones
- Ensure unidirectional impulse flow
- Filter and control impulse transmission
- Convert electrical signals to chemical signals and back

# Impulse Transmission Across Synapse <sub>[12.3.4]</sub>

## What's the Goal?
Convert electrical nerve impulses to chemical signals that can cross the synaptic gap and trigger new electrical impulses in the receiving neurone.

## The Breakdown
{{% steps %}}
### Step 1: Impulse Arrival
Electrical impulse reaches the synaptic knob, triggering calcium channels to open.

### Step 2: Neurotransmitter Release
Synaptic vesicles are stimulated to release neurotransmitters (like acetylcholine or noradrenaline) into the synaptic gap.

### Step 3: Diffusion Across Gap
Neurotransmitter molecules diffuse across the narrow synaptic gap to reach the receiving neurone.

### Step 4: Receptor Binding
Neurotransmitters bind to specific receptor proteins on the membrane of the receiving neurone.

### Step 5: New Impulse Generation
The binding triggers the opening of ion channels, generating a new electrical impulse in the receiving neurone.
{{% /steps %}}

## Visual Summary
**Chemical Transmission Process**: Electrical â†’ Chemical â†’ Electrical
- Energy required from mitochondria for vesicle release and neurotransmitter synthesis
- Process is slower than electrical transmission due to chemical conversion steps
- Neurotransmitters are recycled or broken down after use

# Three-Neurone Reflex Arc <sub>[12.4.2]</sub>

## What's the Goal?
Provide immediate, protective responses to harmful stimuli without waiting for brain processing, preventing serious injury.

## The Breakdown
{{% steps %}}
### Step 1: Stimulus Detection
Sensory receptor detects harmful stimulus (like sharp pin prick) and generates nerve impulse.

### Step 2: Sensory Transmission
Sensory neurone carries impulse from receptor through dorsal root to spinal cord grey matter.

### Step 3: Spinal Integration
Relay neurone in spinal cord receives impulse from sensory neurone via synapse and processes it locally.

### Step 4: Motor Activation
Relay neurone transmits impulse to motor neurone through another synapse in the spinal cord.

### Step 5: Protective Response
Motor neurone carries impulse through ventral root to effector muscle, causing immediate withdrawal movement.
{{% /steps %}}

## Visual Summary
**Reflex Arc Components:**
- **Sensory Neurone**: Carries "danger" signal inward
- **Relay Neurone**: Makes "decision" in spinal cord
- **Motor Neurone**: Carries "action" signal outward
- **Bypass**: Brain is informed but doesn't control the initial response

*Image suggestion: A detailed anatomical diagram showing the path of a reflex arc from a finger touching a pin, through the spinal cord with all three neurones labeled, showing both dorsal and ventral roots.*

âœ… **Quick Check**: Why don't you feel the pain until after you've already pulled your hand away?
ðŸ’¡ **Real-World Link**: This reflex system is why people can jump away from danger (like a snake) before they're even consciously aware of what they saw - the spinal cord response is faster than brain processing.

# Endocrine System Overview and Structure <sub>[12.6.1]</sub>

## What is it?
The endocrine system is a network of ductless glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate body functions.

## Key Characteristics
- Composed of ductless glands (no tubes or ducts)
- Hormones travel through blood circulation
- Targets specific cells with matching receptors
- Works slower than nervous system but has longer-lasting effects
- Cooperates with nervous system to maintain homeostasis

## Primary Functions
**Three Main Hormone Functions:**
- **Reproduction**: Sexual development and reproductive processes
- **Growth**: Physical development and tissue maintenance  
- **Homeostasis**: Maintaining stable internal conditions

**System Operation:**
- Glands secrete hormones into bloodstream
- Hormones circulate throughout body
- Only target cells with specific receptors respond
- Hormone-receptor binding triggers cellular responses
- Feedback mechanisms regulate hormone levels

**Coordination with Nervous System:**
- Both systems maintain homeostasis
- Nervous system: Fast, short-term responses
- Endocrine system: Slower, long-term regulation
- Systems interact and complement each other

- **Slow Wound Healing**: High glucose impairs immune function and circulation

- **Symptoms**: Fatigue, sleep difficulties, confused thinking, fear, emotional instability, fainting, headaches
